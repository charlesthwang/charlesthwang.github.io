Increasing Pet Adoptions with Improved Search

Overview

The Montgomery County Animal Services and Adoption Center provides high-standard sheltering and advocates for reponsible pet care through community outreach.

I worked to redesign the MSASAC search experience, while also giving it a modern look and feel.

Click here to jump down to the final design.

What I Did

  • Usability testing
  • Competitive analysis
  • Wireframing & prototyping
  • Hi-Fi mockup

Timeline

February 2020, 3 weeks

Problem

The current search experience for animals makes it difficult for people to browse and quickly find what they are looking for. This is especially apparent when using the site on a mobile device.

This may cause people to prematurely abandon the site, resulting in missed opportunities for adoptions and fostering.

How might we increase pet adoptions and fostering?

Hypothesis

I believe that getting users to quickly find the animal they are looking for will increase pet adoptions and fostering.

Usability Testing

To get a better understanding of usability issues, I asked five people to go through three realistic task-based scenarios.

(See the tasks and post-session questionnaire here).

After observing and reviewing their actions and comments, I noticed that the majority of issues fell into one of three categories:

  • Visual/content hierarchy
  • Page layout
  • Lack of ability to search/filter

Insights

Visual/Content Hierarchy

Usability tests showed that users would rather see more bigger pictures on the search results page. They also had a hard time digesting the amount of description text with one person stating that it was too narrative. The proximity of the description text between animals added to that frustration.

It was also pointed out that the description is repeated on the animal detail page, so it unnecessarily competes for attention.

In addition, since all the content is in the same font size and weight, there isn't any sense of hierarchy to determine what content is important.

Not only does this increase cognitive load and make the content difficult to scan, but the lack of emphasis on important content means that the call-to-action (CTA) can get lost. In fact, no one was able to complete the task that required clicking on this link.

Page Layout

Users didn't like how the content was displayed in rows. Since tables are best suited for presenting data, the content doesn't work well in this layout.

The layout confused users as they didn't know what actions were possible. One person tried clicking directly on a table row and another clicked on a picture only because the description text didn't contain any links.

Search/Filter

The biggest frustration was the lack of ability to filter the search results. One user was forced to go through each page of results to see all of the available dogs because her apartment had breed/size restrictions.

When asked to find a specific cat available for adoption, users didn't realize they could alphabetize the search results by clicking on the description heading (by default they are organized by ID number). Instead, they resorted to the browser's built-in Find command.

Users also tried using the site-wide search at the top of the page, but even this caused confusion. Since MCASAC's website lives under the larger county website, the search here not only included results for MCASAC, but the entire county website.

Only two users used the "Maximum Search" link towards the bottom of the page. But this also caused confusion because "Maximum Search" meant searching animals at other shelters. Users expected their search to be within MCASAC.

“To me, maximum search means maximum parameters”

Ideation

I looked at other pet adoption websites to see if they showed similiar issues and draw inspiration from what they were doing well.

These were the main takeaways that could ease the frustrations uncovered from usuability testing:

  • Establish a clear visual/content hierarchy so users can easily find information they're looking for
  • Use a grid layout to improve navigation and display animals in digestable chucks
  • Add the ability to filter by animal attributes to allow for speedier searches

Let's examine how this design can ease users' frustrations.

Reorganizing Content and Establishing Scale

Save for the name, breed, and age, the description text was removed from the search results for each animal. Removing the superfluous text allows users to scan the results more quickly.

To further increase scannability (and decrease cognitive load), there is a clear sense of hierarchy and scale in regards to the text. By establishing this visual rhythm, users can more easily discern through size, position, and proximity what pieces of content they should prioritize (e.g. pet name or number of results).

In addition, the CTAs are much more prominent and less likely to be overlooked.

The Grid Layout

Rather than a table, using a grid layout will be more consistent with what users expect from their experience on other websites. Since the description text is redundant, removing it will create the space to allow for a grid layout.

Creating this visual organization will not only help users navigate and digest the content more easily, but also makes it clear that the grid card is clickable. Additionally, a grid layout will allow for a cleaner mobile design, as tables don't play nice when responding to narrower screen widths.

Adding Search/Filter

The inability to filter results was the most notable frustration uncovered from user testing, so I included 5 filter critera (based on a survey on what factors were most important to users when considering a pet).

The ability to sort by either name or ID number is retained, but is now much more evident by a segmented button. There is also a dedicated search box for pets separate from the county site-wide search box.

The Homepage

Currently, the homepage contains the hours of operation, a few social media links and, nothing else.

This is a huge missed opportunity to introduce MCASAC's mission, CTAs, and help guide users to other important areas of the site.

Without any of this, users are left wondering what the site has to offer, or if they are even on the right website to begin with.

To find what they're looking for, users are left to hunt through the navigational menu items. To search for adoptable dogs, for example, users have to know to navigate to Adoption > Adoptable Pets > Search Dogs.

First impressions are important. Giving the impression that a website is easy to use by having a well-designed and organized homepage can garner trust and minimize site abandonment.

For the new homepage, I placed the animal search functionality in the header. This reduces the number of steps users have to take to begin searching for pets.

I also relocated the featured pets to the homepage. Currently, featured pets live at the bottom of the search results pages. Having them on the homepage will increase views and acts as another pathway to the search page.
